		\begin{center}\textbf{Abstract}\end{center}
		\noindent RUNMAXXIN is a system that chooses the music a runner hears, moment by moment, so that the run itself -- not the phone -- stays in focus. The problem it addresses is that a runner's musical needs change continuously with two things that often disagree: what the runner set out to do, and what their body is actually doing. The system reads a short natural-language prompt to capture the runner's goal and mood, and at the same time interprets heart rate and cadence to estimate real effort. A controller fuses these two sources into an acoustic target -- a tempo, an energy level, and an emotional tone -- and a recommender selects songs that match it. Around this statistical core, an explicit symbolic layer enforces the rules that must always hold, most importantly steering the music toward recovery when the runner's heart is overworked. The result is a hands-free, safety-aware musical companion that adapts to both intention and physiology.

		\vspace{5pt}
		\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} natural language processing; physiological sensors; ontology; recommendation systems; sport.
	\end{minipage}
	\end{center}

	\section{Introduction}

	Music strongly shapes how a run feels and performs, yet a runner's musical needs shift continuously with two things that often disagree: the goal they set out to pursue, and the effort their body is actually producing. Reconciling these two in real time, through a decision process that can be inspected and trusted rather than a single opaque model, is the specific problem we address; it is relevant to Artificial Intelligence as a case of multimodal fusion under conflict, not as a sport gadget. Existing approaches each fall short on one axis. Early physiological players such as MPTrain and PAPA~\cite{oliver2006mptrain, oliver2006papa} adapt music to heart rate but require the goal as a numeric target, never in words, and blend adaptation and safety into one learned mapping that cannot be separately inspected or overridden. Knowledge-based recommenders such as COMUS~\cite{song2009comus} reason symbolically over mood and situation, but were built for seated listening, with no continuously measured physiological state and no real-time safety constraint. Modern deep recommenders~\cite{oord2013deep} optimise preference accuracy with large, opaque audio models -- the wrong objective when the binding constraint is the runner's safety. Practical open-source apps~\cite{wu2020heartbpmusic, ray2018heartbeat} merely match song tempo to heart rate, with no stated intent, no safety limit, and no evaluation.

	Our contribution is not a new model but a set of deliberate, testable choices and a critical analysis of the components we reuse. First, for language understanding we adopt a few-shot classifier and compare it both against a rule-based keyword baseline and against the standard alternative of fine-tuning a large transformer, showing the lightweight choice is justified on accuracy and latency in our low-data regime. Second, we do not assume the recommender is correct: we measure that, although it computes a probability over candidate songs, it selects deterministically and violates the runner's physiological effort band in a measurable fraction of cases, and we correct this with a resampling step grounded in standard exploration theory. Third, we keep the hard safety rule as an explicit, queryable check rather than folding it into a learned model, and argue why a lightweight symbolic boundary suits real-time constraints better than a full reasoner. Each point is a modification or analysis of an existing method, evaluated rather than asserted.

	\section{Related Work}

	The idea of adapting music to a runner's body is not new. The earliest and most influential example is MPTrain~\cite{oliver2006mptrain}, which monitors heart rate and step frequency and selects songs whose tempo steers the user toward a target heart-rate curve; its successor PAPA~\cite{oliver2006papa} generalizes this into physiology- and purpose-aware playlist generation. A related line of work, the affective music player of Janssen et al.~\cite{janssen2012amp}, instead uses biosignals to guide a listener toward a desired emotional state rather than a performance goal. These systems established the premise we build on -- that physiological signals should drive music selection in real time -- and rest on sport-science research relating musical tempo and rhythmic entrainment to exertion and performance~\cite{karageorghis2012music, vandyck2015entrainment}. We use that literature not as background but as the quantitative basis of two of our components. To turn a stated running speed into a target musical tempo we rely on the near one-to-one entrainment between step cadence and beat reported by Van Dyck et al.~\cite{vandyck2015entrainment}, keeping the target inside the motivational tempo range identified by Karageorghis~\cite{karageorghis2012music}; to convert raw heart rate into a comparable notion of effort we use the heart-rate-reserve model of Karvonen~\cite{karvonen1957} together with the age-based maximum of Tanaka~\cite{tanaka2001}. The exact formulas we derive from these results are given in the Methodology. In all of these prior systems, however, the user's goal must be given as a number (a heart-rate curve, a mood coordinate), never in words, and adaptation and safety are handled by a single learned mapping with no separately inspectable rule. Our system departs on both points: it takes a short natural-language prompt as the goal, and it keeps the safety logic explicit and separate from the statistical recommender.

	More recent work has moved music recommendation toward deep learning. The seminal approach of van den Oord et al.~\cite{oord2013deep} predicts latent preference factors directly from audio with convolutional networks and remains a reference for content-based recommendation. This direction favors large, data-hungry, and largely opaque models -- a reasonable trade-off when the objective is preference accuracy, but a poor fit for our setting, where the bottleneck is real-time safety rather than fine-grained taste prediction and where interpretability matters. For the language side of our pipeline we instead adopt SetFit~\cite{tunstall2022setfit}, a few-shot method that contrastively fine-tunes a sentence-embedding model and trains a lightweight classification head (Fig.~\ref{fig:setfit-arch}). This matches what our setting actually offers -- only tens of prompts per class: in this low-data regime, fine-tuning a large encoder such as RoBERTa-large~\cite{liu2019roberta} still trails a few-shot Sentence-Transformer by a wide margin (Fig.~\ref{fig:setfit-cr}), which is precisely why we do not follow the more common full fine-tuning route and instead treat the choice as a measured trade-off. The emerging use of large language models to fuse raw sensor streams~\cite{sensorllm2024} is complementary but orthogonal to our aim: we use language only to capture the user's stated intent, and keep the physiological signals as compact numeric features rather than feeding them to a general-purpose model.

	The symbolic side of our system is closest to knowledge-based music recommenders. COMUS~\cite{song2009comus} builds an OWL ontology of mood and situation and uses rule-based reasoning to infer suitable music, explicitly bridging low-level audio features and high-level emotion; more recent neuro-symbolic pipelines~\cite{kutt2026neurosymbolic} combine SPARQL queries over a knowledge graph with a neural component. Our use of an ontology is deliberately narrower, and this is the key distinction. In COMUS the ontology \emph{is} the recommendation engine: the reasoner derives what to play. In our system the recommendation stays statistical, and the ontology acts only as a thin deterministic boundary around it -- it declares the safety threshold that a SPARQL query checks, validates the numbers extracted from text through a SHACL constraint, and screens the recommender's candidates for effort compatibility. The knowledge that COMUS places inside the reasoner, such as which genres suit a given mood, we keep as explicit data outside the graph, reserving the symbolic layer for constraints that must be \emph{guaranteed} rather than \emph{inferred}. This also avoids the runtime cost of a full description-logic reasoner, which is ill-suited to the continuous, high-frequency updates of a running session.

	\begin{figure}[t]

		\caption{In the low-data regime, SetFit reaches high accuracy from a handful of labeled examples per class, where fine-tuning a large transformer (RoBERTa-large) still lags. Adapted from Tunstall et al.~\cite{tunstall2022setfit}.}
		\label{fig:setfit-cr}
	\end{figure}

	On the evaluation side, the standard benchmark for music recommendation is the Million Song Dataset~\cite{bertin2011msd}, but it targets preference prediction over listening histories and does not fit a physiological, safety-constrained task; our catalogue is instead a set of Spotify audio features, and our physiological sessions are simulated -- a limitation we share with much of this literature, where the lack of a standard, shareable physiological-music dataset and small participant counts are recurring problems. For the language component we follow the spirit of few-shot text-classification protocols such as RAFT~\cite{alex2021raft}, reporting macro-averaged F1 against a rule-based baseline on a held-out set. For the recommender, the deficiency we address -- that it can return songs violating the runner's effort band -- is exactly what the safety-recommendation literature formalizes as a safety-violation ratio~\cite{safecrs2026}, consistent with the finding that in health-related recommendation, safety outweighs accuracy as an evaluation criterion. The corrective step we add is not ad hoc either: it is the classical Boltzmann (soft-max) action-selection rule from reinforcement learning~\cite{sutton2018rl}, whose temperature trades exploitation for exploration, applied here to turn the recommender's single fixed choice into a controlled, temperature-scaled sampling among effort-compatible candidates. At the opposite end of the spectrum, practical open-source projects such as heartBPMusic~\cite{wu2020heartbpmusic} and HeartBEAT~\cite{ray2018heartbeat} match song tempo to heart rate directly but include neither language understanding, nor safety overrides, nor any evaluation. In short, prior work either fuses physiology and music without language and without an inspectable safety layer, or reasons symbolically without real-time physiological constraints; our contribution unites a natural-language intent channel, a statistical recommender, and a symbolic safety boundary, and -- unlike the practical systems above -- measures and corrects a concrete safety-violation rate instead of assuming correctness.

	\section{Experimental Setup}

	\subsection{Dataset}

	Two natural-language datasets were written by the authors, one per classifier. The \emph{goal} dataset has three classes (EasyRun, ModerateRun, IntenseRun); the \emph{mood} dataset has five (Neutral, Focused, Energetic, Motivated, Calm). Inputs are short free-text prompts ($\leq 20$ words). Following the few-shot protocol we keep no validation set, and the held-out test sentences are disjoint from training (verified programmatically). Table~\ref{tab:dataset} summarises the splits. Two further resources are used as provided, not modified: a catalogue of 89{,}503 Spotify tracks with audio features (tempo, energy, valence, genre), and six simulated physiological sessions (heart rate, speed, cadence) rather than real recordings. Preprocessing is lightweight: lowercasing and regular-expression number extraction for text; numeric coercion, range filtering and de-duplication for the catalogue. The main biases are the small size of the hand-written NLP sets, the simulated (not measured) physiology, and duplicate tracks in the catalogue.

	\subsection{Training and Evaluation Protocol}

	Both classifiers use SetFit with the \texttt{paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2} sentence encoder, contrastively fine-tuned on sentence pairs and topped with a logistic classification head, trained on a MacBook~M2 in minutes (Table~\ref{tab:training}). We evaluate on the held-out test set with accuracy and macro-averaged F1, and compare against a deterministic keyword-rule baseline (an ablation isolating the contribution of the learned model). The same test sentences and metrics are used for both methods to keep the comparison fair. The rest of the pipeline (controller, recommender, symbolic layer) is rule- or query-based and requires no training.

	\subsection{Results}

	On goal classification the few-shot model reaches $0.778$ accuracy against $0.556$ for the keyword baseline, a $22$-point margin (Fig.~\ref{fig:acc}). The gain is not uniform across classes: per-class F1 (Fig.~\ref{fig:f1}) is $0.87$ for ModerateRun and $0.77$ for EasyRun, but only $0.67$ for IntenseRun, whose recall is $0.50$. On mood the model reaches $0.800$ accuracy (macro-F1 $0.81$). The confusion matrices (Figs.~\ref{fig:cmgoal} and~\ref{fig:cmmood}) localise two systematic errors: intense prompts phrased without technical vocabulary are read as EasyRun, and uncertain mood prompts collapse onto Neutral. Table~\ref{tab:results} reports the aggregate figures, including the macro-F1 and the average intent-extraction latency ($16$~ms), which the plots do not show.

	\subsection{Critical Discussion}

	The few-shot classifier clearly outperforms the rule-based baseline, and the margin \emph{widens} on the larger, harder test set (from a handful of examples to $36$/$40$ per task, the goal gap grows to $22$ points). This supports the contribution stated in the introduction: the learned model generalises to phrasings the keyword rules miss, which is exactly why we preferred it over both a rule baseline and a heavier fine-tuned transformer. The hardest case is IntenseRun: effort expressed without race vocabulary (``give everything until I collapse'') is read as easy, because the training set under-represents jargon-free intensity; the confusion matrix isolates this failure. On mood, the model's tendency to fall back on Neutral when unsure lowers recall on Motivated and Focused. These outcomes are coherent with the few-shot literature: strong from little data, fragile out of distribution. The main limitations are the small NLP datasets, the simulated physiological sessions, and catalogue duplicates; the clearest improvement is to enlarge and balance the training data, especially jargon-free intense and mood examples, which our error analysis identifies as the dominant error source.
